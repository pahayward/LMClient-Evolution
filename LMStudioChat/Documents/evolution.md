# Theory of Evolution

## Overview

The theory of evolution by natural selection is the unifying theory of life sciences, explaining the diversity of life on Earth. First systematically articulated by Charles Darwin and Alfred Russel Wallace in 1859, it describes how populations of organisms change over successive generations through heritable variation and differential reproductive success.

Evolution is defined as change in the heritable characteristics of biological populations over successive generations. These characteristics are expressed by genes and arise from mutation, genetic recombination, and other sources of genetic variation. Evolution occurs when there is heritable variation within a population that influences reproductive success.

---

## Historical Background

### Pre-Darwinian Thought

Before Darwin, most naturalists believed species were fixed and immutable, created by God in their present forms. However, several thinkers laid groundwork:

- **Aristotle** (384–322 BC) classified organisms and noted similarities suggesting common origins, but believed species were eternal and unchanging.
- **Jean-Baptiste Lamarck** (1744–1829) proposed the first coherent theory of evolution in his 1809 *Philosophie Zoologique*, suggesting organisms evolve by inheriting traits acquired during their lifetime (now known as Lamarckism). While the mechanism was wrong, it established that species could change over time.
- **Georges Cuvier** (1769–1832) founded the science of palaeontology and demonstrated that fossils represented extinct species — evidence the living world had changed over time.
- **Charles Lyell** (1797–1875) established in *Principles of Geology* (1830–33) that Earth was shaped by slow, continuous processes over vast time scales — providing the deep time Darwin needed for evolution to work.

### Darwin and Wallace

**Charles Darwin** (1809–1882) spent five years on the voyage of HMS *Beagle* (1831–1836), collecting specimens across South America, the Galápagos Islands, Australia, and beyond. His observations of geographic variation — particularly the distinct finch and mockingbird species on different Galápagos islands — convinced him that species were mutable.

Darwin spent more than twenty years carefully building his case before publishing *On the Origin of Species by Means of Natural Selection* in 1859. The book sold out on its first day and triggered a scientific and social revolution.

**Alfred Russel Wallace** (1823–1913) independently conceived natural selection while suffering from fever in Borneo in 1858. His letter to Darwin prompted them to jointly present their theory to the Linnean Society of London on 1 July 1858 — a landmark date in science.

## Core Mechanisms

### 1. Natural Selection

Natural selection is the process by which heritable traits that increase reproductive success become more common in a population over generations.

**Requirements for natural selection to operate:**

1. **Variation** — individuals in a population differ from one another
2. **Heritability** — traits can be passed from parents to offspring
3. **Differential fitness** — some variants survive and reproduce better than others

Darwin described it with the phrase *"survival of the fittest"* (a term coined by Herbert Spencer), where "fitness" means reproductive success in a given environment — not physical strength.

**Types of natural selection:**

| Type | Effect on trait distribution | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Directional selection | Shifts mean toward one extreme | Increased antibiotic resistance in bacteria |
| Stabilising selection | Reduces variation around the mean | Human birth weight |
| Disruptive selection | Favours both extremes over the mean | Beak size polymorphism in black-bellied seedcrackers |
| Sexual selection | Favours traits that improve mating success | Peacock tail feathers |

### 2. Genetic Variation and Mutation

The raw material for evolution is **genetic variation** — differences in DNA between individuals. Sources of variation include:

- **Mutation** — random changes in DNA sequences caused by errors in DNA replication or damage from radiation, chemicals, or viruses. Most mutations are neutral or harmful; a small fraction are beneficial.
- **Genetic recombination** — the shuffling of gene variants during sexual reproduction (meiosis), generating new combinations of traits.
- **Gene flow** — movement of genes between populations through migration and interbreeding.
- **Genetic drift** — random changes in allele frequencies, especially pronounced in small populations (see below).

### 3. Genetic Drift

Genetic drift is the random change in allele frequencies due to chance events — not selection. It is most significant in small populations, where chance events can dramatically alter gene frequencies:

- **Founder effect**: a small group splits off from a larger population and the new group's gene pool reflects only the founders' alleles.
- **Bottleneck effect**: a population is drastically reduced by a disaster; the surviving gene pool may not represent the original population.

Genetic drift reduces genetic diversity and can fix deleterious alleles — it is an important force in small or isolated populations.

### 4. Sexual Selection

Sexual selection, also described by Darwin, occurs when traits evolve because they improve an individual's ability to obtain mates rather than survive. It operates through:

- **Intersexual selection (mate choice)**: one sex (often female) chooses mates based on traits — e.g., female peafowl prefer males with larger, more elaborate tails.
- **Intrasexual selection (competition)**: members of one sex compete for access to the other — e.g., stags fighting for females.

Sexual selection can drive traits that are costly to survival (a peacock's tail makes him conspicuous to predators) because the mating benefit outweighs the survival cost.

### 5. Gene Flow

Gene flow is the transfer of genetic material between populations. It tends to reduce genetic differences between populations and can spread advantageous alleles across a species' range. Barriers to gene flow — geographic, ecological, or behavioural — are often a precursor to speciation.

## Evidence for Evolution

### The Fossil Record

Fossils provide a chronological record of life on Earth extending back ~3.5 billion years. Key evidence includes:

- **Transitional fossils**: specimens showing intermediate features between ancestral and descendant groups. Examples include *Tiktaalik* (fish-tetrapod transition), *Archaeopteryx* (dinosaur-bird transition), and a complete series of fossil intermediates showing whale evolution from land mammals.
- **Stratigraphic succession**: fossils appear in rock layers in an order consistent with evolution — simpler organisms in older layers, more complex in younger layers.
- **Living fossils**: organisms like the coelacanth and horseshoe crab have changed little over hundreds of millions of years, consistent with their stable environments.

### Comparative Anatomy

- **Homologous structures**: anatomical features with similar underlying structure but different functions — e.g., the pentadactyl (five-fingered) limb shared by humans, bats, whales, and horses — indicate common ancestry and evolutionary modification.
- **Analogous structures (convergent evolution)**: similar function but different structure — e.g., the wings of birds, bats, and insects — evolved independently in response to similar selective pressures.
- **Vestigial structures**: remnants of features that had functions in ancestors — e.g., the human coccyx (vestigial tail), the pelvic bones of whales, and the human appendix.

### Molecular Biology and Genetics

The molecular evidence for evolution is overwhelming:

- **Universal genetic code**: all life uses essentially the same DNA triplet codons to specify the same amino acids — strong evidence of common descent.
- **Shared DNA sequences**: humans and chimpanzees share approximately 98.7% of their DNA sequence. Humans and mice share about 85%.
- **Molecular phylogenetics**: comparing DNA, RNA, or protein sequences between species produces phylogenetic trees that match those derived from anatomy and the fossil record.
- **Endogenous retroviruses (ERVs)**: remnants of ancient viral infections are found at identical locations in the genomes of humans and other primates — they could only be identical if inherited from a common ancestor.
- **Pseudogenes**: non-functional copies of genes exist at the same positions in related species, consistent with common ancestry.

### Biogeography

The geographic distribution of species provides powerful evidence:

- **Island biogeography**: oceanic islands have fewer species than comparable mainland areas, and those species tend to resemble species on the nearest mainland — consistent with colonisation and subsequent evolution.
- **Galápagos finches**: 15 species of finch on the Galápagos Islands, all descended from a single South American ancestor, diversified to fill different ecological niches — a classic example of **adaptive radiation**.
- **Continental distributions**: marsupial mammals are concentrated in Australia and South America — the two continents that were once joined via Antarctica before plate tectonics separated them.

### Direct Observation of Evolution

Evolution has been directly observed in real time:

- **Bacterial resistance**: the evolution of antibiotic resistance is documented continually in hospitals worldwide.
- **Peppered moth**: industrial melanism in *Biston betularia* in 19th-century Britain — dark moths became dominant as soot darkened tree bark, removing camouflage from lighter moths.
- **Galápagos finch beak evolution**: Peter and Rosemary Grant spent decades documenting beak-size changes in *Geospiza fortis* following drought years that altered seed availability.
- **Laboratory experiments**: Richard Lenski's *E. coli* long-term evolution experiment (running since 1988) has documented thousands of generations of adaptive change, including the novel evolution of citrate metabolism.

## Speciation

Speciation is the process by which one species splits into two or more reproductively isolated species. The key concept is **reproductive isolation** — when gene flow between populations ceases, they diverge independently.

### Allopatric Speciation
Geographic isolation (a mountain range, ocean, river) separates populations, preventing gene flow. The populations diverge under different selective pressures and through genetic drift until they can no longer interbreed. This is the most common speciation mode.

### Sympatric Speciation
Speciation without geographic isolation — new species arise within the same geographic range. This occurs through:
- **Polyploidy**: genome duplication events common in plants (e.g., bread wheat *Triticum aestivum* has six sets of chromosomes from three ancestral species).
- **Ecological divergence**: adaptation to different ecological niches within the same area (e.g., apple maggot fly *Rhagoletis pomonella* evolving host-plant races on hawthorn and apple).

### Parapatric and Peripatric Speciation
Speciation in populations that are geographically adjacent (parapatric) or where a small peripheral population diverges from a larger one (peripatric — similar to the founder effect).

## Macroevolution and the Tree of Life

**Macroevolution** refers to evolution at and above the species level — the origin of major body plans, mass extinctions, and the diversification of life over geological time.

### Common Descent

All life on Earth shares a **last universal common ancestor (LUCA)**, estimated to have lived 3.5–3.8 billion years ago. Evidence comes from:
- Universal biochemistry (DNA, RNA, ATP, the genetic code)
- Ribosomal RNA sequences shared by all domains of life
- The fossil record

The three **domains of life** are:
1. **Bacteria** — prokaryotes without a nucleus
2. **Archaea** — prokaryotes, often extremophiles, biochemically distinct from bacteria
3. **Eukaryota** — organisms with a membrane-bound nucleus, including protists, fungi, plants, and animals

### Endosymbiotic Theory

The eukaryotic cell originated through **endosymbiosis**: an ancestral archaeon engulfed bacteria, which became the **mitochondria** (in all eukaryotes) and **chloroplasts** (in plants and algae) we see today. Evidence includes:
- Mitochondria and chloroplasts have their own circular DNA
- They reproduce by binary fission, like bacteria
- Their ribosomal RNA resembles that of bacteria, not eukaryotes

### Major Evolutionary Transitions

Key events in the history of life on Earth:

| Time (approx.) | Event |
|---|---|
| ~3.8 billion years ago | Origin of life / LUCA |
| ~2.7 billion years ago | Evolution of oxygenic photosynthesis |
| ~2.1 billion years ago | First eukaryotes |
| ~600 million years ago | First multicellular animals |
| ~530 million years ago | Cambrian explosion — rapid diversification of animal body plans |
| ~500 million years ago | Plants colonise land |
| ~375 million years ago | Fish transition to land (tetrapods) |
| ~230 million years ago | Origin of dinosaurs and early mammals |
| ~66 million years ago | Cretaceous–Palaeogene extinction (end of non-avian dinosaurs) |
| ~6–7 million years ago | Divergence of human and chimpanzee lineages |
| ~300,000 years ago | *Homo sapiens* appear |

## Modern Evolutionary Synthesis

The **Modern Synthesis** (1930s–1950s) united Darwin's theory of natural selection with Mendelian genetics and population genetics. Key figures included:

- **Ronald Fisher**, **J.B.S. Haldane**, and **Sewall Wright** — mathematical population genetics
- **Theodosius Dobzhansky** — *Genetics and the Origin of Species* (1937) linked genetics to natural populations
- **Ernst Mayr** — the biological species concept and allopatric speciation
- **George Gaylord Simpson** — palaeontology and macroevolution
- **G. Ledyard Stebbins** — plant evolution and polyploidy

### Extended Evolutionary Synthesis

Since the 2000s, researchers have called for an **Extended Evolutionary Synthesis** to incorporate:

- **Epigenetics**: heritable changes in gene expression not caused by DNA sequence changes
- **Developmental plasticity**: how environments can alter developmental trajectories
- **Niche construction**: how organisms modify their own selective environment
- **Horizontal gene transfer**: transfer of genes between organisms without reproduction, common in bacteria

## Evolutionary Misconceptions

### "Evolution is just a theory"
In science, a *theory* is a well-substantiated explanation supported by extensive evidence — not a guess. The theory of evolution is as well-supported as the germ theory of disease or the theory of gravity.

### "Evolution is random"
Mutation is random, but natural selection is not — it systematically favours traits that improve reproductive success in a given environment. The combination of random mutation and non-random selection produces adaptation.

### "Evolution means organisms get more complex"
Evolution has no direction or goal. Complexity can increase, decrease, or stay the same. Parasites, for example, often evolve *simpler* body plans by losing features they no longer need.

### "Humans evolved from chimpanzees"
Humans and chimpanzees share a common ancestor; neither descended from the other. We are cousins, not parent and child.

### "Evolution happens to individuals"
Evolution happens to *populations*, not individuals. An individual cannot evolve during its lifetime (though it can develop and change). Evolution is the change in allele frequencies across generations in a population.

## Relevance and Applications

The theory of evolution has profound practical applications:

- **Medicine**: understanding pathogen evolution is essential for developing vaccines, managing antibiotic resistance, and tracing disease outbreaks. Evolutionary medicine explores why humans are susceptible to certain diseases.
- **Agriculture**: selective breeding and our understanding of genetics derive directly from evolutionary principles. Crop and livestock improvement depends on them.
- **Conservation biology**: evolutionary principles guide strategies for preserving genetic diversity and managing endangered species.
- **Biotechnology**: directed evolution techniques are used to engineer proteins with desired properties — accelerating natural selection in the laboratory.
- **Phylogenetics**: evolutionary trees (phylogenies) are used to classify organisms, trace the origins of pathogens, and understand gene function.

As the geneticist Theodosius Dobzhansky wrote in 1973:

> *"Nothing in biology makes sense except in the light of evolution."*

This remains the guiding principle of the modern life sciences.

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| Allele | A variant form of a gene |
| Fitness | Reproductive success relative to other individuals |
| Gene pool | The total genetic information in a population |
| Genotype | An organism's genetic makeup |
| Phenotype | Observable characteristics of an organism |
| Selection pressure | Environmental factors that affect reproductive success |
| Speciation | The formation of new and distinct species |
| Phylogeny | The evolutionary history and relationships of a species or group |
| Adaptive radiation | Rapid diversification of a lineage into many ecological niches |
| Convergent evolution | Independent evolution of similar traits in unrelated lineages |
